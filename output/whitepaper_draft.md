# Against a Moratorium on Data Centers: A Progressive Case for Social Control Over Retreat

---

## Introduction

The American left is building a coalition against the wrong target. Across the country, progressives have rallied behind moratoriums on data center construction as the centerpiece of their response to artificial intelligence. Senator Bernie Sanders wants a national pause. State legislatures from New York to Wisconsin have introduced sweeping bans. More than 230 organizations have signed on to a call for a federal moratorium.[1] The winner of the New Jersey 11th Congressional District Democratic primary, Analilia Mejia, campaigned on a moratorium as a core plank.[2] In the second quarter of 2025 alone, moratorium advocates delayed or killed data center projects valued at approximately $100 billion.[3] The movement is real, the frustration driving it is legitimate, and the problems it identifies are serious. Electricity bills are climbing. Developers are cutting deals behind nondisclosure agreements. The wealth generated by AI is flowing almost entirely to a handful of technology executives. But moratoriums do not solve any of these problems. They delay them.

This report examines each major argument for a moratorium and finds that the policy fails on every dimension progressives care about. The local impacts that motivate moratoriums can be addressed more effectively through energy reform and direct regulation of data center developers. State-level moratoriums do not slow the development of AI capabilities; they displace construction to jurisdictions with weaker labor and environmental protections. A national moratorium would be worse still, ceding strategic ground to international competitors, undermining the safety research that progressives rightly want to see funded, and foreclosing the transformative benefits that AI could deliver in medicine, science, and public services if its gains were broadly shared.

The moratorium movement has correctly identified a set of problems. It has proposed the wrong solution. The alternative is an agenda that combines energy abundance, safety mandates, worker power, and shared ownership of the gains from automation. That agenda is harder to build than a moratorium. It is also more durable, and more consistent with the progressive tradition of demanding social control over new productive forces. The Wisconsin moratorium bill is instructive in this regard: its conditions include 100 percent renewable energy requirements, prevailing wage mandates, bans on nondisclosure agreements between developers and government, mandatory public reporting, and prohibition of subsidies without accountability.[4] Every one of those conditions can be enacted as standalone regulation, without a moratorium, and with the added benefit of applying to data centers that actually get built in Wisconsin rather than to the empty lots left behind when developers move to Texas.

## 1. Local Impacts Are Addressable Without a Moratorium

### 1.1 Energy Costs

The concern that data center expansion will raise electricity costs for residential ratepayers is the most substantive argument in the moratorium coalition's case. Electricity rates in the United States increased 21.3 percent between 2021 and 2024.[5] PJM Interconnection, the regional transmission organization serving the mid-Atlantic states where data center construction has been most concentrated, saw capacity auction prices increase roughly tenfold between 2024 and 2025, adding an estimated $20 per month to residential bills in affected areas.[6] Progressives are right to insist that the technology industry, not working families, bear these costs.

The question is whether a construction ban is necessary to achieve that, and the answer, based on the experience of several states that have already acted, is no. Oregon's POWER Act (HB 3546) creates a separate utility customer category for electricity loads exceeding 20 megawatts, with direct cost assignment, ten-year minimum contracts, and mandatory payments regardless of actual consumption.[7] Virginia requires data center operators to post upfront collateral and meet minimum demand obligations before grid interconnection.[8] Anthropic, one of the leading AI laboratories, has publicly committed to paying 100 percent of the grid upgrade costs associated with its data center projects, bringing net-new electricity generation online to match its consumption, and investing in curtailment systems that reduce power draw during periods of peak demand.[9] These interventions address the cost-shifting problem directly, and they do not require halting construction.

Data center expansion, if managed through regulatory frameworks of this kind, could accelerate rather than impede progress toward a cleaner and more resilient electricity grid. The United States has built through comparable demand surges before. During the 1950s, U.S. electric generating capacity grew at 9.5 percent annually, driven by rising demand from household appliances and industrial expansion. President Truman warned in 1951 that supply was becoming "increasingly short throughout the country."[10] Rather than restricting new demand, the federal government invested in generation and transmission. The result was cheaper electricity, broader access, and a grid that powered four decades of broadly shared prosperity. Demand growth paired with good policy produces a virtuous cycle of expanding capacity and falling real prices. Demand growth paired with construction bans produces scarcity, price spikes, and ratepayer harm.

The deeper energy problem confronting the United States is not that data centers consume too much electricity. The country has made it extraordinarily difficult to build new electricity generation of any kind. NEPA litigation delays energy projects by an average of 3.9 years, even though federal agencies ultimately prevail in 71 percent of challenges; ten organizations are responsible for 48 percent of all energy-related NEPA cases filed between 2013 and 2022.[11] The interconnection queue for new power plants averages six or more years. Over 350 gigawatts of generation capacity sits waiting in these queues for approval, enough to meet anticipated AI energy demands many times over, but only one in five queued projects survives the process.[12] Annual transmission line construction has collapsed from 4,000 miles in 2013 to approximately 500 miles today.[13] A moratorium does nothing to solve these problems and, by reducing the political urgency of permitting reform, risks entrenching them.

The progressive response to energy cost concerns should be to require data center developers to build and pay for their own electricity generation, and to enact the permitting reforms that allow them to do so using clean energy sources. Solar, wind, geothermal, and nuclear power are all technically viable options for powering data centers. They are difficult to build in the United States for reasons that have nothing to do with data centers and everything to do with a permitting regime that progressives should want to fix on its own merits. As the Breakthrough Institute has documented, the NECEC transmission line, designed to deliver Canadian hydropower to 1.2 million New England homes and reduce carbon emissions by 3.6 million metric tons annually, was delayed 21 months by legal challenges, increasing project costs by nearly 50 percent and adding approximately $500 million in costs to Massachusetts ratepayers.[14] Passing a moratorium instead of permitting reform amounts to a concession that the United States should never meaningfully expand its electricity supply, a position that is untenable for any movement that takes climate change, reindustrialization, or economic equality seriously.

### 1.2 Water Use

Data center water consumption has received outsized attention relative to its actual scale. All data centers in the United States combined consume approximately 0.2 percent of national freshwater withdrawals. Artificial intelligence workloads specifically, which account for roughly one-fifth of total data center electricity demand, are responsible for approximately 0.008 percent of national freshwater consumption.[15] Even aggressive growth projections that assume a tripling of data center capacity by 2030 would bring on-site water consumption to approximately 0.12 percent of national freshwater, an amount comparable to eight percent of the water consumed by the American golf industry.[16] In Maricopa County, Arizona, which is frequently cited as a region burdened by data center water demand, data centers collectively consume 0.12 percent of county water while golf courses in the same county use 3.8 percent and generate roughly one-fiftieth of the tax revenue per gallon consumed.[17]

Several widely circulated media claims about data center water use do not hold up to examination. The frequently repeated assertion that a single AI-powered email consumes a full bottle of water was based on a methodology that assumed ten AI queries per email and applied the water footprint of the most carbon-intensive electricity sources in the country to all queries regardless of actual power source.[18] Claims that data centers are draining local aquifers have, in at least one documented case, been traced to construction-phase sediment runoff rather than operational water consumption at a facility that had not yet begun operating.[19] These distinctions have been lost in media coverage that often conflates data centers with cryptocurrency mining operations, which have substantially different resource profiles.

Individual facilities in water-stressed communities should still face scrutiny in siting decisions, and developers should invest in closed-loop cooling and recycled water infrastructure. But the claim that data centers as a category represent a significant threat to American water supplies is not supported by the available data, and a national or state-level moratorium is not a proportionate response to a problem of this magnitude.

### 1.3 Environmental Impacts

The environmental case for a moratorium rests primarily on climate concerns: 56 percent of data center electricity is currently sourced from fossil fuels.[20] This is a real problem, but the remedy is to change the energy source rather than to ban the consumer. Data center operators are among the largest private purchasers of clean energy in the world. Microsoft's 20-year power purchase agreement will bring the Crane Clean Energy Center (formerly Three Mile Island Unit 1) back online, adding 835 megawatts of zero-carbon nuclear generation to the grid.[21] Fervo Energy is constructing a 400-megawatt geothermal facility specifically to serve data center demand.[22] In both cases, the appetite for electricity from data centers is the demand signal that makes new clean energy investment financially viable. Moratoriums eliminate that signal.

Waste heat recovery represents an additional opportunity that moratoriums foreclose. In Stockholm, 30 data center facilities have been integrated into the city's district heating network, converting waste heat at 40 to 60 degrees Celsius into residential heating supply. Germany's Energy Efficiency Act mandates waste heat recovery for new data center construction.[23] A moratorium enacted in the name of environmental protection would prevent precisely the kind of integrated energy planning that turns data centers from liabilities into community assets.

A moratorium that pushes data center construction from states with strict renewable energy standards to states powered primarily by natural gas could, perversely, increase the total carbon emissions associated with AI. The modeling section of this report demonstrates that moratoriums shift construction from blue states, which tend to have cleaner grids, toward Gulf Coast and Sun Belt regions with heavier fossil fuel reliance. The environmental case for a moratorium undermines itself.

## 2. State-Level and Local Moratoriums Do Not Slow AI Capabilities Growth

Moratoriums are premised on a theory of change that does not survive empirical scrutiny. The theory holds that restricting data center construction in a given jurisdiction will reduce the total quantity of AI compute deployed and thereby slow AI capabilities growth. In practice, the opposite occurs. Hyperscale data center developers operate globally and allocate capital based on regulatory environment, grid access speed, labor costs, and land availability. When permitting becomes difficult or impossible in one state, planned capacity shifts to states and countries that offer more favorable conditions. The result is geographic redistribution of AI infrastructure, not reduction.

New York's S.9144, the most expansive moratorium proposal in the country, would prohibit all data center permits for facilities consuming 20 megawatts or more until the Department of Environmental Conservation completes a Generic Environmental Impact Statement and the Public Service Commission finishes a rate impact analysis, a process likely to take three years or more.[24] The bill would not delay a single frontier AI training run. It would redirect billions of dollars in construction spending, thousands of union construction jobs, and tens of millions in annual tax revenue to jurisdictions that impose fewer conditions on developers. Every dollar and every job moved out of New York is a dollar and a job that New York can no longer attach conditions to. The Wisconsin moratorium bill, whose conditions represent a genuinely thoughtful progressive framework for data center regulation, produces the same perverse outcome: the regulations it envisions would apply to no data centers at all, because the moratorium would ensure none are built within its jurisdiction.

Companies like Meta, Microsoft, Amazon, and Google collectively committed approximately $400 billion in capital expenditure for 2025 alone.[25] That spending follows the path of least regulatory resistance. A progressive state that enacts a moratorium is, in practice, subsidizing a conservative state's tax base while surrendering its own regulatory authority over the industry.

### Modeling Results

To quantify these dynamics, this report develops a quarterly simulation model running from Q1 2026 through Q4 2032, integrating three modules: a geographic displacement model based on multinomial logit allocation with agglomeration dynamics, a pipeline-based capacity accumulation model with region-specific construction timelines and queue congestion feedback, and a hardware compute module adapted from Epoch AI's GATE framework that maps quality-weighted data center capacity to frontier training run capability.[26] The model tracks 13 market regions (10 domestic, 3 international) characterized by grid interconnection speed, fiber density, cost structure, policy environment, water availability, and labor market conditions. Three scenarios are modeled: a no-moratorium baseline, a "currently considering" scenario in which states with active proposals pass them, and a broad scenario in which every Democratic trifecta state except Virginia enacts a moratorium.

Under the broadest moratorium scenario, total U.S. data center capacity reaches 53.7 gigawatts by 2032, compared with 54.7 gigawatts under the baseline, a peak shortfall of 2.5 percent. Blue-state share of national capacity falls from 55 percent to 45 percent; red-state share rises from 24 percent to 30 percent; international share increases from 8 percent to 11 percent. Under the narrower "currently considering" scenario, the peak shortfall is just 0.3 percent. In both cases, investment does not vanish. It relocates to Texas, the Gulf Coast, and the Sun Belt within two to four quarters, with a fraction leaking to international jurisdictions.[27]

The more consequential finding concerns AI capabilities timelines. Hardware compute for frontier training runs depends on data center capacity, GPU density and performance, power efficiency, and algorithmic improvements. Moratoriums affect only the first of these. GPU performance gains compound at roughly 30 percent annually on semiconductor industry timelines that have nothing to do with where data centers are located.[28] Algorithmic efficiency gains, which double effective compute approximately every 12 months, are entirely unaffected by construction policy.[29] In aggregate, hardware deployment accounts for approximately 27 percent of total AI capabilities growth, and software improvements account for 73 percent. Under every moratorium scenario modeled, every hardware compute milestone is reached in the same quarter as the baseline. The delay to AI capabilities growth is zero.

This result holds even under stress testing. If reallocation delays are doubled, international leakage tripled, or queue congestion in receiving regions substantially increased, the effect on capabilities timelines remains negligible. Moratoriums constrain a factor, the physical location of data centers, that accounts for a small and diminishing share of the inputs to AI progress. A state legislature cannot vote to slow TSMC's chip fabrication roadmap, Google DeepMind's algorithmic research, or the global semiconductor supply chain. It can vote to move a construction project from one county to another, and the modeling confirms that this is all moratoriums accomplish.

### What Moratoriums Cost

The displacement documented above carries concrete costs. Data center projects generate substantial tax revenue for host communities: the reopening of the Crane Clean Energy Center in Pennsylvania is projected to create approximately 3,400 jobs, over $3 billion in state and federal taxes, and $16 billion in GDP over the life of the agreement.[30] In Henrico County, Virginia, $60 million in data center tax revenue funded an affordable housing trust fund expected to support 750 homes over five years.[31] In New Albany, Ohio, a data center tax abatement was linked to community grants for STEM education and local infrastructure.[32] In New Jersey, incentive agreements include up to $250 million in tradeable tax credits conditioned on matching ecosystem investments and a $20 million startup fund.[33] Moratoriums eliminate the revenue, the leverage, and the community benefits simultaneously.

Data center construction also provides significant employment in the building trades. Individual projects employ between 1,500 and 6,400 construction workers, with union electricians earning approximately $59.50 per hour, reflecting a 46 percent premium over non-union wages.[34] The Wisconsin moratorium bill itself mandates prevailing wages for data center construction, an implicit acknowledgment that these projects generate exactly the kind of high-quality employment the labor movement has fought to create. Banning construction eliminates these jobs.

Most fundamentally, a state that hosts data center infrastructure has leverage over the companies operating within it. That leverage disappears when those companies build elsewhere. States with data centers on their soil can require safety investments, mandate community benefit agreements, impose reporting requirements, ban nondisclosure agreements between developers and local officials, and subject operators to environmental regulation. A state without data centers can do none of these things. A moratorium is a unilateral surrender of regulatory authority over an industry whose trajectory will shape the twenty-first-century economy.

## 3. The Benefits of AI Capabilities Growth

A moratorium on data center construction is, at bottom, a moratorium on the growth of AI capabilities. Proponents frame it as a targeted intervention against an infrastructure nuisance, but the computational resources housed in data centers are the substrate on which AI systems are trained, deployed, and improved. Restricting the substrate restricts the technology. Whether that restriction is desirable depends on what AI capabilities are actually producing.

In healthcare, AI systems are saving lives at measurable scale. The COMPOSER algorithm, deployed at UC San Diego Health, reduced sepsis mortality by 17 percent across a study of 97,559 hospital stays, a result that extrapolates to thousands of preventable deaths annually if adopted across the U.S. hospital system.[35] The Sepsis ImmunoScore, authorized by the FDA in April 2024, became the first AI diagnostic tool approved for sepsis, a condition that kills approximately 350,000 Americans each year.[36] AI-assisted breast cancer screening has demonstrated a 17.6 percent improvement in diagnostic accuracy, reducing both false negatives that delay treatment and false positives that subject healthy patients to unnecessary biopsies.[37] Google DeepMind's AlphaFold has been used by more than three million researchers worldwide to predict protein structures from amino acid sequences, a capability that accelerates drug discovery for diseases from malaria to Alzheimer's at a pace previously considered decades away.[38]

In scientific research, the impact extends beyond the medical domain. DeepMind's GNoME system identified 2.2 million new materials with predicted stability, expanding the known set of viable materials by an order of magnitude and opening pathways in battery technology, superconductors, and semiconductor design.[39] GenCast, an AI weather prediction system, outperforms the best traditional forecasting methods on 97.2 percent of evaluation targets, with direct implications for hurricane preparedness, agricultural planning, and climate adaptation in the communities most vulnerable to extreme weather.[40] The U.S. Treasury Department has used AI systems to prevent more than $4 billion in fraudulent payments, resources that would otherwise have been extracted from programs serving low-income Americans.[41]

In education, randomized controlled trial evidence indicates that AI tutoring systems outperform traditional active learning instruction for students who lack access to private tutoring, a gap that falls disproportionately along lines of income and race. Khan Academy's Khanmigo platform has reached over 700,000 users, the majority in public school districts.[42] In climate science, AI models are improving the resolution and accuracy of the climate projections on which adaptation policy depends, enabling regional planning that was previously impossible at useful timescales. Each of these applications runs on the same data center infrastructure that moratorium advocates propose to prohibit.

Progressives who support a moratorium should reckon with these tradeoffs directly. A moratorium on data centers is a moratorium on faster sepsis diagnoses in emergency rooms, on accelerated drug discovery for neglected diseases, on weather forecasts that protect farming communities, on fraud prevention that safeguards public benefits, and on educational tools that narrow achievement gaps. The benefits of AI are not evenly distributed today, and ensuring equitable access is a legitimate policy goal. But a moratorium does not redistribute AI's benefits. It reduces them.

## 4. A National-Level Moratorium Is Extremely Undesirable

### 4.1 The Strategic Cost

If state-level moratoriums merely redistribute data center construction within the United States, a national moratorium would redistribute it globally. RAND Corporation analysis projects that global AI data center power requirements will reach 68 gigawatts by 2027 and 327 gigawatts by 2030.[43] That demand will be filled. A national moratorium would determine only that the infrastructure filling it is located outside the United States. China added 429 gigawatts of electrical generation capacity in 2024, roughly fifteen times the U.S. addition, and has built 27 nuclear reactors in the past decade while the United States built two.[44] Data center development is increasingly flowing to nuclear-capable nations including France, Japan, and the United Arab Emirates.

The progressive framing of this problem should follow the logic that Representative Ro Khanna and others have articulated. International competition in AI is not an abstraction for working people. When the United States falls behind in AI infrastructure, the consequences include American job losses across the technology supply chain, reduced leverage over global AI governance standards, and diminished capacity to ensure that the most powerful AI systems in the world are developed under safety constraints that reflect democratic values. The countries that host AI infrastructure will be the countries that write the rules for its use.

### 4.2 Safety Research Requires Compute

Senator Sanders has acknowledged the risks posed by advanced AI systems, including the possibility that sufficiently capable AI could pose existential-scale risks.[45] This concern is shared by many researchers in the AI safety community and warrants a serious response. A moratorium is the wrong instrument for a specific and underappreciated reason: the most promising approaches to AI safety are themselves computationally intensive.

Mechanistic interpretability, the leading technical approach to understanding what AI systems are doing internally and why, requires running large-scale analyses across the same infrastructure used to train the models being studied. Anthropic's research team extracted tens of millions of interpretable features from the Claude 3 Sonnet model using sparse autoencoders, a technique that required scaling from small transformer models to frontier-scale systems and consumed substantial computational resources at each stage.[46] Alignment research, red-teaming, model evaluation, and the development of AI oversight tools all require large quantities of compute. This is not incidental. Safety research scales with the models it is trying to make safe. As models grow larger and more capable, the compute required to understand, test, and constrain their behavior grows proportionally. A moratorium on the infrastructure that provides that compute slows safety research alongside capabilities research, a result that should trouble anyone who takes AI risk seriously.

The alternative is direct: mandate that AI companies allocate a meaningful fraction of their computational resources to safety work, fund a federal alignment research program at scale, and impose regulatory requirements on model development that ensure safety is not treated as a discretionary expense. These interventions target the risks that motivate concern about AI capabilities growth. A moratorium targets them only indirectly, only temporarily, and at enormous cost to the other benefits AI delivers.

### 4.3 The Case for a Treaty

If the United States is serious about reducing the global risks posed by advanced AI, the path runs through international cooperation rather than unilateral retreat. A national moratorium would not prevent China, the United Kingdom, the UAE, France, or any other country from continuing to expand AI infrastructure. It would, however, substantially weaken the United States' position in any future negotiation over AI governance by removing its standing as the world's leading AI developer.

The progressive position should be to pursue binding international agreements on AI safety, compute governance, and risk management. Such agreements have precedent in the nuclear domain, where the Non-Proliferation Treaty and the Comprehensive Nuclear-Test-Ban Treaty established multilateral constraints on a dangerous technology while preserving the ability of signatories to pursue civilian applications. An AI safety treaty could establish mutual obligations around compute thresholds for safety testing, shared reporting on frontier model capabilities, and joint investment in alignment research. Negotiating such a treaty from a position of industrial strength produces different outcomes than negotiating from a position of voluntary self-imposed weakness. The United States should be at the table with leverage, not with empty data center lots.

## 5. Automation, Social Control, and the Progressive Opportunity

The moratorium movement's deepest concern is not about electricity bills or water tables. It is about power: the fear that artificial intelligence will automate large categories of human labor, that the resulting wealth will flow to a narrow class of technology owners, and that working people will be left with nothing. This fear is well-founded. But the political response it calls for is not a pause on building infrastructure. It is the construction of institutions that determine who benefits from the technology once it arrives.

The left has confronted this situation before. The factory system concentrated wealth in the hands of industrialists, and the labor movement responded by organizing trade unions, winning the right to collective bargaining, and establishing workplace safety standards. The postwar economy automated millions of agricultural and manufacturing jobs, and progressives responded by building Social Security, expanding public education, passing the GI Bill, and fighting for a minimum wage. The mechanization of agriculture destroyed more jobs, proportionally, than AI is projected to eliminate in the next two decades, and the political response created the most broadly shared prosperity in American history. In each case, the progressive movement demanded social control over new productive forces and insisted that their gains be distributed. It did not demand that the forces be stopped.[47]

Artificial intelligence presents the same confrontation at larger scale. If AI systems can perform an increasing share of routine cognitive labor, the progressive project should be to ensure that the resulting productivity gains translate into shorter working hours, universal economic security, and democratic governance over the conditions of work. That project requires building institutions now, during the period of transition, rather than enacting a moratorium that delays the transition without building anything. A moratorium substitutes the comfort of a pause for the harder work of institutional design, and when the pause expires, the same problems remain without the institutions to address them.[48]

The following four pillars constitute a substantive progressive agenda for AI, one that is more ambitious than a moratorium and more likely to produce durable results.

### 5.1 A Social Wealth Fund

The most direct mechanism for ensuring that AI's gains are broadly shared is public ownership of a stake in the companies developing it. A social wealth fund, capitalized through a combination of market capitalization taxes on the largest AI firms (paid in newly issued equity) and dedicated compute taxes, would allow the public to participate in the financial upside of AI development. The fund's returns would be distributed as a universal dividend, creating a permanent link between productivity gains from automation and household income. Norway's Government Pension Fund Global holds over $1.7 trillion in assets and distributes returns to the Norwegian public. Alaska's Permanent Fund pays annual dividends to every state resident from oil revenue. In both cases, public ownership of a share of an extractive industry's returns has proven administratively feasible, politically durable, and economically significant.[49] AI is the twenty-first century's extractive industry. The resource being extracted is human knowledge, labor, and attention. The public is entitled to a share of the returns.

### 5.2 Safety Mandates

AI companies should be required to allocate a specified percentage of their total computational resources to safety research, including mechanistic interpretability, alignment testing, red-teaming, and model evaluation. This requirement should be paired with federal investment in a public alignment research program operated at a scale analogous to the national laboratory system that supports nuclear safety. State-level regulation along the lines of the RAISE Act should establish transparency requirements, pre-deployment safety evaluations, and enforceable standards for model release. Stricter model release regulations, particularly for open-weight releases of frontier-scale models, should ensure that safety evaluation keeps pace with capability development. These mandates directly address the risks that moratorium advocates cite. A moratorium addresses them only by accident, only temporarily, and only for the duration of the pause.[50]

### 5.3 Empower Labor

The automation of cognitive work will reshape the labor market over the coming decades, and workers must have institutional power over that process. Sectoral bargaining frameworks should give unions and worker representatives a formal role in negotiating the terms under which AI systems are introduced in their industries, including pace of adoption, retraining provisions, transition support, and compensation structures. Worker co-determination on corporate boards, following the German Mitbestimmungsgesetz, would give employees a vote on automation decisions at the highest level of corporate governance. Serious, federally funded experimentation with a four-day workweek would test whether productivity gains from AI can be channeled into reduced working time rather than unemployment. A federal jobs guarantee would provide a floor of dignified employment as labor markets adjust. The United Kingdom's TUC has proposed an AI Employment Rights Bill that incorporates many of these elements, and European sectoral bargaining agreements already provide templates for negotiated automation that protects workers while permitting technological adoption.[51] Collectively, these mechanisms ensure that automation, when it comes, strengthens rather than undermines the position of labor.

### 5.4 Energy Abundance and Permitting Reform

The energy constraints that motivate much of the moratorium movement are real, but they are the product of permitting and regulatory failures that progressives should want to fix on their own merits. Federal permitting reform, including reducing NEPA litigation timelines from the current average of 3.9 years to the 150-day judicial review window proposed in the SPEED Act, would accelerate clean energy construction across the board.[52] Requiring data center developers to finance and build their own generation capacity using solar, wind, geothermal, and nuclear power would eliminate cost-shifting to ratepayers while creating the largest demand signal for clean energy investment in American history. The Institute for Progress's proposal for Special Compute Zones on federal land, with coordinated permitting, security requirements, and co-located clean energy generation, provides a template for federal facilitation of data center construction that serves the public interest.[53] The supply chain investments and reindustrialization policies that progressives have long championed, from domestic semiconductor manufacturing to critical minerals processing, are the same investments that accelerate AI infrastructure. These agendas are complements.

Under a future progressive administration, the most transformative AI scenarios may also warrant direct public ownership of frontier AI laboratories, ensuring that decisions about technologies of civilizational consequence are made through democratic processes rather than corporate boardrooms.

---

## Conclusion

The moratorium movement is a symptom of a genuine failure: the failure of American political institutions to manage the rapid expansion of a transformative technology in ways that serve the public interest. Communities have every reason to be frustrated. The concerns about rising electricity costs, corporate secrecy, environmental degradation, and concentrated wealth are real, and they demand a forceful political response.

A moratorium is not that response. It surrenders leverage over the very companies progressives want to hold accountable. It displaces jobs and tax revenue to states with weaker labor protections. It slows the safety research necessary to manage AI risk. It forecloses transformative benefits in healthcare, science, and education. And it replaces the hard work of building permanent institutions with the temporary comfort of a pause that solves nothing once it expires.

The conditions embedded in the Wisconsin moratorium bill reveal what progressives actually want from AI policy: no cost-shifting to residential ratepayers, prevailing wages, public transparency, renewable energy requirements, and accountability for developers. These are achievable goals. They are achievable through regulation, through worker power, through shared ownership, and through the kind of energy abundance agenda that this country proved it could build seventy years ago. They do not require banning construction. They require governing it.

The progressive tradition, at its best, has never been a tradition of retreat from new productive forces. It has been a tradition of demanding that their gains belong to everyone. Artificial intelligence is the most consequential productive force of this century. The left should meet it with the ambition the moment demands.
